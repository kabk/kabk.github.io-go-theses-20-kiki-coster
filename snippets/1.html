a. Medicalization of Health

 

To instruct and educate physicians and regulate health insurance, one needs “standards that guide the assessment of whether people are ill or not,” and thus, a crucial discussion in the philosophy of medicine regards the conceptualization of health and disease.( 1) That distinction is particularly hard to make, in part because of the variety of the human population.

Moreover, it is debated whether and—

to what extent—concepts of disease are socially constructed. Exploring these distinctions is essential, as they influence when and where people seek medical treatment, and whether society regards them as “sick” ( including whether they are granted medical treatment) .

 

As Tristram Engelhardt

stated,

 

the concept of illness serves not only to “describe and explain but also to take action. It indicates a state as undesirable andto be overcome.”( 2) ( 3)

 

“The typically modern tendency of the medicalization of the notion of health is above all manifest in the relative neglect of the very concept of health in theoretical discussion. Contemporary medical science and consciousness are characteristically concerned with disease and illness and their treatment rather than with the articulation of the conditions which define the goal and criteria of the success of such treatment. Most people today view health as a medical concept and thus accept the physician’s monopoly of the decision-making procedures regarding significant parts of their lives. Some critics suggested there is a paradox in medical definition of health.”( 4)

 

Plato ( ca. 427-347 b.C.) considered medicine to be an art ( techne) that serves personal values. Medical skill should solely consist of the application of the right therapeutic means ( aid) to achieve the restoration of health. However, health itself ( as the end of treatment and cure) is not to be determined by medical practice. The physician should resist the temptation to define the nature of health in medical terms.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

These matters lie beyond medical expertise because health is not a medical matter. Health is a philosophical view of the “physis of man, his essential nature, both as a biological entity and as a social being.” Medicine determining the “ends of treatment” would make it an autonomous practice, and that is exactly what Plato warned against.

 

The medical practice should contain to its instrumental function and resist the autonomous urge; only then can it maintain its rational character. Once medicine renounces its moral and social principles, it may slip into malpractices like over-treatment ( such as usage of therapeutic techniques that go beyond the elementary treatment of localized diseas.) ( 6)

“The physician knows only “the nature of health and disease”, but that does not necessarily put him in a position to judge whether health or illness is the more terrible to a man.( 5)

 

Ancient greek medicine viewed disease as something that arises from a disharmony in the body rather than from interference of external force, and thus, it should be managed in that same order. Disease was considered to be a natural phenomenon ( rather than divine punishment) and, as mentioned before, called for therapeutic “techne.”

 

The ancient Greeks developed the physiocratic school of thought. This concept of health, which directs the therapeutic treatment towards an “end,” is derived from a concept of human physis. This definition has gradually lead to a concentration on the patient’s body. This development has caused an ambiguity of progress in medical science. Magical accounts of disease were replaced by a liberated concentration on the body

(and the development of a rational method of diagnostics and treatment). However, this development has also lead to the separation of medical practice from other dimensions of life. This means that social and environmental factors are no longer taken into consideration. Plato criticized this overly narrow definition of physis in purely somatic terms ( relating to the body).
