a. Panopticism and the

discipline society

 

In the 1960s and 1970s, however, skepticism of authority emerged, questioning the paternalistic attitude of politics. Foucault, in Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison uses the Panopticon—an architectural design by Jeremy Bentham, that enabled the examining gaze to see everyone present at all times—as a diagram, projecting it onto parts of society to analyze power relations and models of governing ( Foucault 1991a).

The concept of the Panopticon—as it was used in ( health) institutions, education, medicine and law—was used by Foucault as a metaphor for the gaze.(25) Not only the literal architectural application of cell division and suggestive observation but also time division, the birth of statistics (observation, assessment, classification), the development of ‘the dossier’ and management of time and body through ‘the exercise’ were adopted.

 

These panoptic systems often remain hidden because they are intertwined with the everyday aspects of life, which is also what makes them so powerful.( 26) Daily life has been invaded with mechanisms of watching and being watched, and when everybody can potentially be under surveillance, people will internalize control, morals, and values. Michel Foucault called the society that uses this way to govern control by imposing normality a disciplinary society, i.e., training people to obey a code of behavior and using punishment to correct disobedience.( 27)

 

 

This disciplinary society has seen a development towards technocratic approaches to governing (control of society by an elite of technical experts). ( 28) ( 29) The power structures, as described by Foucault and Frissen, are everywhere; medicine, the media, advertising, law, education, mass-production.(30) The silent message is to conform to the

norm to be accepted.

 

The political discourse that imposes normality is present in, for example, the mass production of “ready-to-wear” clothing ( this engages with bodily politics, because it the same standardization of the bodies it values in order to design clothes), the design of our cities, the big data market, capitalism, and also into our social relationships. An example of this can be drawn to advertising strategies propagating what to look like, what to eat, what to wear, when to marry etc ( 31)
