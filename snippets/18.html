d. Embodiment thesis

 

Modern medicine is meticulous in its conceptualization of the body as mechanical working parts and the evaluation of bodily functions but fails to acknowledge the body as an interconnected totality—as proposed by, for example, Deleuze and Guattari.( 84)

 

An essential factor of the articulation of recognition of a state as being undesirable (ill), one also has to acknowledge bodily consciousness and what that means for their subjective experience( 86) The physiology of the body certainly affects and sets limits to the different ways we experience and interpret our being and, consequently, our conceptualization and articulation of phenomena surrounding ill health. ( 87) ( 88)

 

Therefore we have to take into account individual structures of (embodied) experience and consciousness. Not only is this an important factor when it comes to the articulation of requests for help and healthcare, it also proves a weak spot in our supposed autonomy of thought.

 

A challenge to the ideas of Plato was (that some knowledge is universal, a common set of ideas, conferring identity and meaning to material objects) is Immanuel Kant (1724–1804).Kant claimed that our understanding of the world is shaped (only) by our senses. And that it is not (necessarily) the underlying (unseen) causes of the phenomena we observe, that shape our understanding.

 

Our perception is thus subjective. Kant also believed in a world of a priori forms (beyond mind and matter); some of these forms seem to be pre-programmed in the brain. The idea of Hume is in line with that of Kant, claiming we cannot achieve a true conception of the self. Our conception exists solely of a “bundle of sensations” that we connect to formulate the idea of the self. In other words, an objective concept of the self is impossible. According to Rene Descartes, the mind was distinct from matter but could influence matter. The mind could control the brain (and thus, the body) via the pineal gland. *

The ideas of philosophers like Hume and Kant still resonate today. In modern philosophy, embodied cognition holds that a person’s cognition is influenced by one’s body (beyond the brain itself). Embodied perception is used to describe the way the body affects our interpretation of the world. Many aspects of cognition—mental constructs (language, concepts and categories) and the performance of cognitive tasks (reasoning or judgment)—are shaped by aspects of the body like the sensorimotor system.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

For example; it has become increasingly clear that psychological stressors can lead to physical symptoms (somatization) involving the nervous, endocrine, and immune systems. And physical sensations, in its turn, can lead to psychological reactions: scientists have found that gut bacteria produce neurotransmitters such as serotonin, dopamine and GABA, all of which play a key role in psychological disorders (many antidepressants increase levels of these same compounds). Light therapy is used as a way to boost our serotonin.

And while psychosomatic disorders (such as certain depressive disorders) still pose a mystery to doctors, it is clear that mental and physical states have a causal relationship.

(The absence of an empirically identifiable meeting point between the non-physical mind (if there is such a thing) and its physical extension (if there is such a thing) has proven problematic in determining the way psyche and soma interrelate.)

 

Even though the exact science behind the phenomena remains a mystery; institutions like big marketing organizations have gratefully used the previous findings to establish a whole new field of research that analyzes the effect of dynamic Sensorimotor experiences (sensory input) on our (customer) behavior and psychology.( 88b) Not only is this strategy used by architects, but it is also used in retail, politics, (packaging) design, etc. Articles like “Psychology of spatial retail design and in-store architecture”, “Lighting as a Seduction Tool for the Restaurants”, “How product packaging affects buying decisions”, “How to make people click your video; clickbait design” are plently.

 

Unconsciously we are influenced by many factors that we are not even aware of. We live in the illusion that we make choices out of free will, however, we are constantly manipulated by institutions that influence our language, environment, spaces, and sensory perceptions. ( 88c) Albeit very subtly.

In the Dutch TV show MindFuck, illusionist Victor Mids “predicts” choices. Afterward, he explains how he has subtly influenced the test subjects by making small adjustments to their living environment prior to the experiment.

One of the tests showed boxer Rico Verhoeven. He was to knock down several test subjects of a certain color. Mids would predict which one he would punch and which one he would skip. After the test, Mids demonstrated how he had manipulated Verhoeven by placing certain objects in the colors of the test-subjects in the dressing room. Examples of these are countless. Another example is the floorplan of Ikea. ( 88d) These arrows and alternative paths might give you a sense of control, but this is an illusion, since you are essentially, deliberately guided past certain items.( 88e)

From this perspective, the influence of large authorities is rather disturbing. Pharmaceutical companies, for example, provide a gut feeling about certain things that may be quite normal, such as “red skin”. Perhaps it is just red skin, or it might be a dangerous bacteria that needs to be treated with antibiotics, just in case..
