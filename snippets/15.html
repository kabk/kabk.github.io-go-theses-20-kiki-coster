a. Language and healthcare

 

Amongst these critics are Lawrie Reznek (1987) and Rachel Cooper (2002), who claim that naturalist approaches do not reckon the values that shape our judgement and thus the way we use the concepts “health” and “disease”. ( 68) ( 69)

“A value-free account of disease cannot be given, some have argued because diseases are defined not by their causes, but by their effects”

 

(Reznek) —

and their effects gain significance within a cultural context. ( 70)The same can be said about disabilities (such as the normative essentialization of (disabled) bodies caused by architectural imposed normal-values).

 

The explanation of illness concepts usually shapes the response to them. The modern doctor mainly observes and listens to their patients, relying on their empirical observations and on the patients accounts of what is wrong: illnesses and diseases are identified because they involve a failure of a function, a pain that is considered abnormal, a deformity, or the threat of premature death.

 

The complicating factor here, is that concepts indicating phenomena of ill-health are generally descriptive and evaluatory. (Engelhardt 1975). Joseph Margolis and H. Tristram Engelhardt, have argued that personal judgments regarding proper function, normal pain, and normal lifespan are usually made with reference to culture-dependent values, and that these definitions should thus be understood in terms of particular cultures and their ideologies (Margolis). ( 71) ( 72) ( 73)

 

Peter Sedgewick states that

“whether the body functions adequately depends on its environment and the demands it has to meet, and these demands, in turn, depend on the conventions and expectations of a given society.”( 74)

Being a feature of the body is not enough to qualify something as an illness. According to Skillington, it is plausible that health is indivisibly linked to belief structures and society’s pressures to achieve, conform, and be normal(disciplinary society), suggesting that illness is only an illness if one cannot meet the requirements of functioning suggested by society. For example; is a person with lactose intolerance only sick if they live in areas where dairy products are part of the traditional diet? Is a person considered disabled only when they have to navigate an ablist environment?( 75)

 

The shared language between patients and healthcare providers enables gathering information to make diagnoses, explaining treatment strategies, and ensuring understanding and joint decision-making (informed consent). However, since medical science does not provide us with a language in which we can carry out a philosophy of health, two problems arise:

 

Manipulatable value-judgments:

1. The use of language when it comes to societal attitudes contributing to health-behavior has proven to be a crucial factor. (Shared) Value concepts (judgments) are rooted in the framework underlying modern medicine. Disease is a divergence from these (social) norms. However, how does one determine unanimous agreement on conceptualization and articulation of phenomena that we designate as illness factors?

To what extent are societally and culturally prescribed values and theoretical positions reflected in (lay) people’s perceptions and articulation of health? i.e., science, philosophy, pharmaceutics, and economics have, as mentioned before, played a significant role in the definition of health and illness, so, to what extent is ‘objective’ language the product of these influences (like Foucault questioned).

 

Embodied cognition thesis:

 

2. Language influences how people think and how they view the world; mental and social constructs are embedded in language to describe thoughts, interpret meaning, and interact with others. In this sense, a language is a unit of cognition and, therefore, an essential ingredient of health and care. However, to what extent does the physiology of the body affect the way we experience and interpret our being?
