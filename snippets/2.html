b. Psychosomatic medicine and Holism

 

Plato stated that we should concur false medical autonomy and somatic medicine by a holistic approach to health, and by subjecting it to morals and politics. The ‘whole’ that should be considered the object of medicine would consist of the soul ( nature which the art of rhetorics takes as its object) and the body ( nature which is the object of medicine). The organ that needs treatment should be viewed in the context of the whole body, and the body as part of a psychosomatic unity of a person ( whose soul causally affects and is affected by the body).

( 7) ( platonic-dualism).

Plato’s causal psychosomatic relation is bi-directional: bodily pain may result in madness and vice versa.

According to Plato, a part of medical treatment is thus verbal. Psychotherapy is even considered a requirement for the effectiveness of medicine. The relationship between patient and doctor is preferably cooperative and dialogical rather than paternalistic.

Interestingly enough, Plato did maintain a paternalistic view of what was to be considered a valuable life (one that is worth being treated), but stated that the wearer of this paternalistic gaze is not the physician. As such, he (rhetorically) questions whether life is always better than death or wether some better never get up from a sickbed.

 

 

 c. Health and politics

 

Plato fought against the medicalization of health by approaching the concept of health in political terms. He stated that health is not only about physical well-being and moral virtue; it is also dependent on the social role of the individual in society.

Each person has certain strengths and weaknesses which would determine their “social role”. One must know their role and can do so by introspection ( rationalism). Each soul-part corresponds to a virtue, role, class and pleasure.

 

For example; the “rulers” class would contain the virtues of wisdom and knowlegde (for the community, corresponding to their soul quality of “rationality” and gaining pleasure from philosophy. Plato claimed that the soldier and the athlete have different kinds of health and, as such, should maintain a different regimen. ( 8) Health here is a value-laden and socially dependent concept. ( Republic 19lb-d)

 

Moreover, Plato stated that there is work assigned to everyone ( roles), and therefore no one has the leisure to be sick; only those who would still be able to perform their social role following their treatment, were considered worthy of treatment.

 

“...only those who would still be able to perform their social role afterwards, were considered worthy of treatment.”

 

Otherwise, they would be of no use to themselves or the state. An individual can, thus, be seen as healthy only if he can fulfill his function in his community.

This view corresponds to Plato’s political notion of health that relates to a person leading a worthwhile life and is characteristic of the moral constraints that should guide the therapeutic intervention of medicine. The government should be constructed to get the best out of its citizens.

Socrates—however; almost everything we know about Socrates is known to us through Plato’s dialogues—argues how in the perfect Kallipolis ( Republic), there should be no intermingling between social classes. The rulers here, are the ones that determine what is just and unjust and others are to follow. Each role has its own virtue and the quality of a good “ruler” is to be able to “discover the true nature of things”. Justice and morals would keep them from serving in their own interest.
