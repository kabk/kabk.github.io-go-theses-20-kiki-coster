e. Naturalistic accounts of health

 

The concept of health has received less (philosophical) attention than disease and illness, perhaps because it raises even more complicated issues than the concepts that describe its supposed absence or because as Havi Carel suggested “illness is often understood as a physiological process that falls within the domain of medical science and is thus outside the scope of philosophy”(something Plato had warned against already) This rather narrow definition of health would be a ‘state’ which medicine aims to restore, and is opposite to be “unhealthy” (rather than disease)(Kass 1975). (According to this definition, medicine should not include aesthetic surgery or terminations of pregnancy or anything which does not restore health) ( 59) A naturalist approach would be to define health as being a product of a functional biology.( 60)

 

But perhaps the concept of health is more complex than that of (the mere absence of) disease and should we, like Plato stated, understand health, not as a medical concept necessarily but a positive state of well-being that can be (to some extend) achieved and retained by taking care of oneself. (Not necessarily pandering to his claim that health should be defined by politics) This view is in line with the current definition of health as described by the Constitution of the World Health Organization (WHO): health is a “state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.” (WHO 1948) ( 61)

 

The typical case in healthcare nowadays is when a person feels ill, the medical profession is able to detect and treat underlying (pathological) disease, and society attributes the sick role. ( 62) As said before, modern medicine primarily focusses on biological, physical causation of phenomena (diseases causing illnesses). Thus a crucial question is whether the judgments we make concerning our biology are also normative. According to Christopher Boorse, the current dominant discourse views disease concepts as deviant from (biologically) natural and normal functioning for a human being,

“involving empirical (observatory) judgments about human physiology and normative judgments about human behavior or well-being”

 

(Bloomfield 2001, Boorse 1975, Ereshefsky 2009, Culver and Gert 1982, Thagard 1999).

 

That means that we have “common sense” beliefs and expectations about the natural functioning of and scientific theories of human biology, and we make judgments about whether some particular condition or way of life is unnatural, undesirable or failing to flourish (ethics). This thus, involves normative criteria.

Leon Kass and Christopher Boorse, stated that “if certain physiological, biological and psychological functions can be identified as natural or essential to humans, then their absence can be used to define disease states”, and in turn that health is the absence of disease.

 

“Disease is an internal state which impairs normal functional ability” caused by the environment (Boorse 1997). (Boorse 1997).( 63)

According to them, one can specify functions that are essential humans, and thus it is possible to define disease in terms which are not relative to a particular culture or set of values. They argue that the role of medicine is to define (underlying) biological criteria so that we can define diseases and distinguish essential applications of treatment. “Naturalists” focus on what is biologically natural and normal functioning for human beings, and by doing this they appeal to a supposed evolutionary account of “species-typical levels of species-typical functions appropriate for age and gender” (Boorse 1976). ( 64) ( 65)

They claim here, to rely on biological science to define health and disease. However, critics state that they essentially only rely on an equation of statistical and theoretical normality (what they call “natural state” of the organism)(Ereshefsky 2009). Biology does not give us norms, since there is nothing absolutely standard in “species design.”(Sober 1980). And however modernist efforts may lead us to believe otherwise; standard physiology does not provide these norms either(Ereshefsky 2009).

Robert S. Wachbroit, professor in epidemiology and public health, claims this is not in the least part because “physiological accounts typically provide idealized and simplified descriptions of organs and their functions, but not of their natural states” (Wachbroit 1994).( 66)

Another criticism of the naturalist approach is that their ideas are riddled with the assumption that biological fitness, survival and reproduction are the goals of human life. And that medicine is consequentially only considered to be interested in biological fitness. This would mean that medicine is not concerned with to other human goals and values that make no difference in terms of biological fitness such as relief of pain.( 67)
